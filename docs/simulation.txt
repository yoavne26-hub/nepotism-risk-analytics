Demand side

Reward allocation

Then your promotion / raise model chooses who gets rewarded.

You said:

I’d make this slightly more structured:

top producers demanding reward = candidate pool for reward
model ranks them
a capped percentage get promotion / raise

Quitting rule

Employees who did not receive what they wanted quit.

remaining candidate pool
hiring model again
scenario-specific behavior again

What changes across the 3 scenarios

This is the heart of the simulation.

1. Merit-based
Hiring
merit dominates
connection has weak effect
Promotions / raises
driven mostly by performance and tenure
little or no favoritism
Outcomes expected
better long-run production
lower unfair turnover
lower anomaly score
weaker nepotism network concentration
2. Moderate favoritism
Hiring
mix of merit and connection
Promotions / raises
performance still matters, but connected employees get some edge
Outcomes expected
decent short-term production
moderate anomaly concentration
some productive employees quit after being passed over
moderate network clustering
3. High nepotism risk
Hiring
connection dominates a large share of decisions
Promotions / raises
close family / school / city ties heavily influence rewards
weaker employees can be protected
Outcomes expected
lower long-run production
more quitting among productive non-connected workers
more churn
stronger anomaly signals
stronger nepotism clusters

This is nicely aligned with your sources: connected selection can produce lower competence and lower organizational performance, especially where discretion is high.
